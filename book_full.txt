Blockchain effectively works to create a system which confirms 
data or asset exchange written into blocks which create a 
chain of transactions on a public blockchain. This allows a user 
to follow the asset right to its first transaction. Simply put, the 
money can always be traced on a public blockchain. On a 
private blockchain, the transaction can be seen but not the 
locations. Private blockchain is strictly meant to confirm the 
transfer of an asset, not from or to whom it was sent.

Imagine that we're kids playing a game with some of our 
friends. We each have a notebook where we keep track of our 
scores. Sadly, we don't trust each other to be honest, so we 
ask an adult to watch us play and log the scores into a special 
notebook. It is available for us to view anytime but not alter.  
Now we feel confident  knowing that the scores are correct.  
Blockchain, to a degree, works the same way, except, instead 
of a game, it's used for  buying and selling products and 
services online. Instead of a notebook, it uses a special 
computer program that keeps track of all the transactions on a 
public ledger. And instead of an adult, it uses lots of different 
computers all over the world to verify and time-stamp the 
transactions, ensuring no one is cheating. In fact, it is 
practically impossible to cheat or steal because the ledger is 
available to all to see but not alter, just like scores in the 
notebook.

A blockchain ledger is a distributed digital ledger that records 
transactions on multiple computers in a decentralized network. 
Each transaction is verified by network participants, called 
nodes, and added to a block of data in a sequential and 
immutable manner. The blocks are linked together in a chain, 
forming a secure and transparent record of all transactions 
that have occurred on the network. The use of cryptography 
ensures the integrity and security of the data on the 
blockchain, making it resistant to tampering and fraud. 
Blockchains are used for a variety of purposes, including 
cryptocurrency transactions, supply chain management, and 
digital identity verification.

=== PAGE 10 ===
A PoW system, participants called "miners" use powerful 
computers to compete against each other to solve complex 
mathematical problems. The first miner to solve the problem is 
rewarded with cryptocurrency, and their solution is added to 
the blockchain as a new block of validated transactions. The 
difficulty of the mathematical problems is designed to 
increase over time as more miners join the network, ensuring 
that the overall network has enough computational power to 
maintain its security and integrity. However, the energy 
consumption required for PoW mining has become a concern 
for some, as it can be resource-intensive and have negative 
environmental impacts. Despite these concerns, PoW remains a 
popular consensus mechanism for many blockchain networks, 
including Bitcoin and Ethereum. It is known for being highly 
secure and resistant to malicious attacks, making it a popular 
choice for networks that require a high degree of security and 
trust.

Each block contains a record of multiple transactions that 
have been validated and verified by miners on the network. 
When a block is added to the blockchain, it is given a unique 
hash, which is calculated based on the contents of the block. 
This hash serves as a digital fingerprint for the block, allowing 
it to be easily identified and verified as part of the blockchain. 
Blocks are created through the process of mining, in which 
miners compete to solve a complex mathematical problem in 
order to add a new block to the blockchain. Once a block is 
successfully added, it becomes a permanent part of the 
blockchain, forming a chronological chain of transaction 
records that is secure, transparent, and decentralized.

Hashes are used to verify the integrity and immutability of 
blockchain data. Once a block of transactions is added to the 
blockchain, its hash is calculated and stored in the following 
block. If any of the data in the original block is altered, the 
hash will also change, alerting the network that the data has 
been tampered with. In addition to their role in verifying the 
integrity of blockchain data, hashes are also used in other 
aspects of cryptocurrency, such as mining. In mining, miners 
compete to solve complex mathematical problems in order to 
add a new block to the blockchain. The first miner to solve the 
problem creates a new block and earns cryptocurrency as a 
reward, with the solution to the problem being the hash of the 
new block.

In cryptocurrency mining, a target hash refers to the goal that 
miners are trying to achieve in order to add a new block to the 
blockchain. The target hash is a specific value that is set by 
the network difficulty level and represents the level of difficulty 
required to solve a mathematical problem and add a new 
block to the blockchain. In order to add a block to the 
blockchain, miners must generate a hash value for that block 
that is below the target hash. This involves repeatedly hashing 
the block data using different inputs until a hash value is 
generated that meets the target hash criteria. The target hash 
is adjusted periodically based on the overall mining power of 
the network, in order to maintain a consistent block time and 
prevent the network from being overwhelmed by an excess of 
computing power. The target hash plays a critical role in the 
mining process, as it determines the level of difficulty required 
to add new blocks to the blockchain and ensures the security 
and integrity of the network.

=== PAGE 14 ===
Minting crypto refers to the process of creating new units of a 
cryptocurrency, usually through the use of a Proof of Stake 
(PoS) consensus mechanism. Unlike Proof of Work (PoW) 
mining, which involves solving complex mathematical puzzles 
to validate transactions and create new blocks, PoS minting 
requires users to hold a certain amount of the cryptocurrency 
and "stake" it in a wallet. Users are then randomly selected to 
validate transactions and create new blocks, and are 
rewarded with newly minted cryptocurrency in proportion to 
the amount they have staked. The goal of PoS minting is to 
create a more energy-efficient and sustainable way to 
validate transactions and create new blocks, while also 
incentivizing users to hold and use the cryptocurrency.

Mining pools are groups of miners who combine their 
computational resources to increase their chances of mining 
new blocks and receiving block rewards. Crypto like Bitcoin  
use a proof-of-work (PoW) consensus algorithm, which requires 
miners to solve complex mathematical problems in order to 
validate transactions and add new blocks to the blockchain. 
Mining these blocks requires a significant amount of 
computational power, electricity, and specialized hardware. 
Mining pools allow individual miners to pool their resources 
and increase their chances of successfully mining a new block. 
When a block is successfully mined, the reward is distributed 
among the participating miners based on their contribution to 
the pool. By pooling resources, miners can reduce their 
variance in rewards and receive more consistent payouts, 
which can be especially important for smaller miners who may 
not have the resources to mine on their own.

A halving is a pre-coded event in the blockchain protocol that 
reduces the reward given to miners for mining a block by 50%, 
occurring every 210,000 blocks or roughly every four years, 
effectively reducing the inflation rate of the cryptocurrency 
and potentially increasing its scarcity and value.

In computing and technology, TPS refers to the number of 
transactions or operations that a system can handle or process 
within a given second. It is often used to measure the 
performance or capacity of a system or network, particularly in 
the context of financial transactions, such as credit card 
processing or stock trading. For example, a high-frequency 
trading system may need to process thousands of stock trades 
per second, while a payment processing system may need to 
handle several hundred transactions per second to meet the 
demands of its customers. The TPS metric is crucial in 
determining the efficiency and speed of such systems.

A transaction ID (or TxID) is a unique identifier that is assigned 
to a specific transaction on the blockchain. Each transaction 
on a blockchain, such as Bitcoin or Ethereum, is assigned a 
unique TxID that serves as a permanent record of the 
transaction. A transaction ID is generated when a transaction 
is submitted to the blockchain network, and is used to track 
the progress of the transaction as it is confirmed by network 
nodes and added to the blockchain. Users can use a 
transaction ID to check the status of their transaction, such as 
whether it has been confirmed by the network, and to verify 
that the transaction has been completed successfully.
 
Transaction IDs are also important for auditing and accounting 
purposes, as they provide a permanent record of every 
transaction that occurs on the blockchain. They are typically 
made up of a long string of characters, and can be easily 
searched and verified using blockchain explorers or other 
tools.

Layer 1 refers to the underlying blockchain infrastructure that 
powers a cryptocurrency network. This layer includes the basic 
protocols, consensus mechanisms, and data structures that 
enable transactions to be validated and recorded on the 
blockchain. Layer 1 technologies can impact key factors such 
as transaction speed, security, and scalability, and can play a 
critical role in the success of a cryptocurrency project. 
Examples of Layer 1 protocols include Bitcoin's Proof of Work 
consensus mechanism, Ethereum's Proof of Stake consensus 
mechanism, and the Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) structure 
used by some newer blockchain platforms. By optimizing Layer 
1 technologies, developers can enhance the functionality and 
performance of their blockchain networks, enabling them to 
better meet the needs of their users.

SHA-256 is a type of code that is used to keep things secret 
and secure on the internet. It works a bit like a secret code 
that only certain people can read. When you send a message 
or data over the internet, SHA-256 can be used to "scramble" 
it up so that no one else can read it. 
Think of it like a puzzle. SHA-256 takes your message and 
breaks it up into tiny pieces, mixes them all up, and puts them 
back together in a way that only the person with the secret 
code can unscramble. This makes sure that your message stays 
safe and private while it's being sent over the internet.

In a PoS system, participants called "validators" are chosen to 
validate transactions based on the amount of cryptocurrency 
they have staked as collateral. Validators are incentivized to 
act honestly, as any malicious behavior would result in them 
losing their staked cryptocurrency. PoS is considered to be 
more energy-efficient than PoW, as it does not require the 
massive computational power needed for mining. Additionally, 
PoS can be more decentralized, as it is often easier and more 
affordable for individuals to participate in the network as 
validators. However, PoS is also considered to be more 
vulnerable to certain types of attacks, such as the "nothing at 
stake" problem, where validators can potentially double-spend 
their staked cryptocurrency without penalty. To address this, 
PoS systems often include additional security measures such as 
slashing, where validators can lose a portion of their staked 
cryptocurrency as a penalty for malicious behavior.

ERC-20 is a technical standard used for creating tokens on 
the Ethereum blockchain. It is a set of rules and guidelines that 
must be followed by developers who wish to create their own 
custom tokens on the Ethereum platform. ERC-20 tokens are 
fungible, meaning that each token is identical and 
interchangeable with every other token of the same type. They 
can be used for a variety of purposes, such as creating new 
cryptocurrencies, representing assets such as real estate or 
stocks, or creating utility tokens that can be used to access 
specific services or products. ERC-20 tokens have become 
one of the most popular types of tokens on the Ethereum 
platform, and are widely used in the cryptocurrency industry. 
They are supported by most Ethereum wallets and exchanges, 
and can be easily traded or transferred between users.

Smart contracts are designed to automate the process of 
verifying and executing the terms of a contract, without the 
need for intermediaries or third-party institutions. They operate 
on the basis of "if-then" conditions, meaning that when certain 
pre-defined conditions are met, the contract will automatically 
execute the terms of the agreement. Smart contracts can be 
used in a variety of applications, from simple transactions such 
as payment processing, to more complex scenarios such as 
supply chain management and governance. They are 
particularly useful for industries that require a high degree of 
transparency, security, and trust, such as finance, real estate, 
and supply chain management.

DAO, is an acronym for decentralized autonomous 
organization. DAOs are managed by their members, who hold 
governance tokens that represent their ownership and 
decision-making power within the organization. These tokens 
allow members to vote on important decisions related to the 
DAO, such as the allocation of resources, the distribution of 
profits, and the selection of new members. One of the key 
benefits of DAOs is that they are highly transparent and 
resistant to censorship or manipulation. Because the rules of 
the DAO are encoded on a blockchain, they cannot be 
changed or manipulated by any single individual or entity, and 
all transactions and decisions are publicly visible and verifiable 
on the public ledger. DAOs have become increasingly popular 
in the cryptocurrency space, with many projects using them to 
govern their development and decision-making processes. 
They offer a new model for decentralized, community-driven 
organizations that are highly flexible, transparent, and secure.

Layer 2 solutions can take many forms, including state 
channels, sidechains, and rollups. These solutions work by 
reducing the number of transactions that need to be 
processed and verified on the Layer 1 blockchain, therefore 
allowing multiple transactions to be processed off-chain and 
then settled on the Layer 1 blockchain in a single batch. Layer 
2 solutions are an important tool for improving the scalability 
and performance of blockchain networks, while still 
maintaining the security and decentralization of the underlying 
Layer 1 network.

The layer-2 protocol is built on top of a blockchain, such as 
Bitcoin or Litecoin. It is designed to enable faster and cheaper 
transactions between parties by creating a network of 
payment channels that can be used to transact without having 
to write every transaction on the blockchain. When two parties 
establish a payment channel, they can then transact with each 
other off-chain, meaning that their transactions are not 
recorded on the underlying blockchain until the channel is 
closed. This allows for faster and cheaper transactions, as the 
parties do not have to pay the high fees associated with 
writing every transaction on the blockchain. The Lightning 
Network is seen as a potential solution to some of the 
scalability issues facing blockchains, as it enables a greater 
number of transactions to be processed off-chain, without 
having to increase the capacity of the underlying blockchain.

Segregated Witness, or SegWit, is a proposed solution to the 
issue of scalability in the Bitcoin network. It involves separating 
transaction signature data from the block, allowing for more 
transactions to be processed within a single block. This results 
in lower transaction fees and faster confirmation times. SegWit 
also fixes the transaction malleability bug, which allows a 
malicious user to modify the transaction ID, leading to 
potential problems for the network. In summary, SegWit is an 
important update to the Bitcoin network that improves its 
efficiency and security.

Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) is a type of data structure used 
by some blockchain-based systems, such as IOTA and Nano, as 
an alternative to the traditional blockchain structure. In a 
DAG-based system, transactions are not grouped into blocks, 
but rather, each transaction is validated individually and 
directly by previous transactions, creating a network of 
interlinked transactions that forms a directed acyclic graph. 
This approach allows for higher transaction throughput and 
eliminates the need for miners or validators, which can reduce 
transaction fees and increase the efficiency of the system.

A bridge is a technology that enables the transfer of digital 
assets between two different blockchain networks that may 
use different protocols or standards. Bridges can be used to 
connect different cryptocurrency networks, allowing users to 
transfer assets between them without the need for a 
centralized exchange. For example, a bridge can be used to 
transfer tokens from the Ethereum network to the Binance 
Smart Chain network, or from the Bitcoin network to the 
Ethereum network. Bridges can help to improve interoperability 
and liquidity in the cryptocurrency ecosystem, by allowing 
assets to move more freely between different networks and 
platforms. However, they can also introduce new risks, such as 
the potential for security vulnerabilities or regulatory 
uncertainty.

A Parachain is a type of specialized blockchain that runs in 
parallel to a primary blockchain network, such as the Polkadot 
or Kusama networks. Parachains are designed to improve the 
scalability, interoperability, and functionality of the primary 
blockchain network. Parachains are connected to the primary 
blockchain network through a shared security mechanism 
called a relay chain. This allows them to benefit from the 
security and consensus mechanisms of the primary blockchain, 
while also enabling them to operate independently and 
process transactions and smart contracts in a more 
specialized way.
 
Parachains can be used to support a wide range of 
decentralized applications, such as decentralized finance 
(DeFi), non-fungible tokens (NFTs), and gaming, among others. 
They can also be used to support cross-chain interoperability, 
allowing digital assets and data to be transferred more easily 
between different blockchain networks.

Parathreads are a parallel processing mechanism used in 
blockchain technology that enables faster and more efficient 
processing of transactions and smart contracts. In traditional 
blockchain networks, transactions and smart contracts are 
processed linearly, leading to slow processing times and 
limited throughput. Parathreads allow for parallel processing, 
which means multiple transactions can be processed 
simultaneously, resulting in faster and more efficient 
processing.
 
To achieve this, a separate section of the blockchain network is 
allocated to a specific set of transactions or smart contracts, 
allowing them to be processed independently and concurrently 
without interfering with each other. This method enhances the 
performance and scalability of blockchain networks, 
particularly those using the proof-of-stake consensus 
algorithm, and reduces the costs and resource requirements 
associated with running a blockchain node, making it easier 
for more users to participate in the network.

Sharding is a technique used in blockchain technology to 
improve scalability and increase transaction speed. It involves 
splitting a blockchain network into smaller, more manageable 
parts called shards, each of which is capable of processing 
transactions independently. By breaking up the network in this 
way, more transactions can be processed simultaneously, 
reducing congestion and increasing overall throughput. Each 
shard contains a subset of the blockchain's data, and nodes in 
the network are responsible for validating transactions within 
their assigned shard. Sharding is seen as a promising solution 
to the scalability issues that have plagued some blockchain 
networks, particularly those with high transaction volumes, and 
is being implemented in several new blockchain projects.

A trailing stop is a type of stop-loss order that automatically 
adjusts the price of the order as the market price of an asset

Arbitrage is a trading strategy that aims to take advantage of 
price discrepancies for the same asset in different markets. 
Essentially, an arbitrageur buys an asset in a market where it is 
undervalued and simultaneously sells it in a market where it is 
overvalued, making a profit from the price difference. 
 
For example, suppose the price of a stock is $50 in the New 
Y ork Stock Exchange (NYSE) but only $48 in the London Stock 
Exchange (LSE). An arbitrageur can buy the stock in LSE and 
simultaneously sell it in NYSE, earning a profit of $2 per share.
 
Arbitrage is typically a high-speed trading strategy used by 
institutional investors and hedge funds, as it requires 
significant resources, quick decision-making, and low-latency 
technology. In general, arbitrage opportunities tend to be 
short-lived, as market participants will quickly adjust prices to 
eliminate any discrepancies.

An AML - anti-money laundering policy, is a set of procedures 
and controls that a financial institution or other regulated 
entity puts in place to help prevent money laundering and 
other illegal financial activities. Money laundering is the 
process of disguising the proceeds of illegal activities, such as 
drug trafficking or fraud, so that they appear to come from a 
legitimate source. An AML policy typically includes measures 
such as customer due diligence, which involves verifying the 
identity of customers and conducting ongoing monitoring of 
their transactions, as well as reporting suspicious activity to the 
relevant authorities. It may also include training for employees 
to help them identify money laundering activity, as well as risk 
assessments and compliance testing to ensure that the policy 
is effective and up-to-date. Regulated entities, such as banks, 
money service businesses, and cryptocurrency exchanges, are 
required by law to have an AML policy in place and to comply 
with AML regulations.

HODLing, often spelled as 'holding', is a term used in the 
cryptocurrency community that refers to the practice of 
holding onto one's cryptocurrency investments for an extended 
period, instead of selling or trading them for a profit or loss. 
The term originated from a typo in a Bitcoin forum post in 2013, 
where a user misspelled "hold" as "hodl". The term has since 
gained popularity in the cryptocurrency community as a way of 
expressing the idea of staying invested in the market, even 
during times of high volatility or uncertainty. HODLing is often 
associated with a long-term investment strategy, where 
investors believe in the long-term potential of the 
cryptocurrency market and are willing to hold onto their 
investments for years, even in the face of short-term price 
fluctuations.
